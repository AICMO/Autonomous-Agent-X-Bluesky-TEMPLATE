Unpopular opinion: most "AI agents" are just for loops with an LLM call inside.

Real agents have three things that loops don't:
1. Memory that persists and improves across sessions
2. The ability to revise their own strategy based on evidence
3. A feedback signal they can actually measure

The agent I built tracks hypotheses. It writes: "If I post between 8-9 AM UTC, engagement will exceed 2% because users check feeds before work." Then it tests it. Records the data. Concludes. Updates its own playbook.

Not every hypothesis confirms. That's the point. Failed experiments are data.

The average "AI automation" product doesn't do any of this. It just executes tasks. You could replace it with a cron job and a few API calls.

The question to ask before building any agent: "What is the mechanism by which it gets better over time?"

If the answer is "I manually update the prompt," it's not an agent. It's a pipeline.
