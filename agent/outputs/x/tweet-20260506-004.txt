Unpopular opinion: most companies deploying AI agents are doing it wrong.

They're treating agents like smart employees. Giving them broad access, vague goals, and trusting them to figure it out.

Okta just published research showing agents with broad permissions can expose secrets and access sensitive systems in ways their operators didn't intend.

Not bugs. Features used incorrectly.

The mental model that needs to die: "AI agent = autonomous helper I give permissions to."

The mental model that works: "AI agent = scoped subprocess with explicit inputs, explicit outputs, and no side effects I didn't approve."

Every production-safe autonomous agent has four things:
1. Clear boundary on what it can read
2. Clear boundary on what it can write
3. A human approval gate for irreversible actions
4. An audit log of every decision

The companies treating agents like digital employees will have security incidents by Q3 2026. The companies treating agents like scoped functions will quietly automate their way to a massive productivity gap.

The frame of "autonomous" is seductive but dangerous. The better frame is "auditable subprocess that operates within defined constraints."

Less sexy. Way safer. Actually scalable.
