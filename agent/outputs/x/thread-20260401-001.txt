Agents are being deployed into production faster than security teams can evaluate them.

This is the biggest unspoken risk in agentic AI right now.

Thread on what's happening and why governance is the unlock — not the blocker.

---

In March 2026, Galileo released Agent Control — an open-source governance layer for AI agent behavior.

The three attack surfaces they're targeting:

1. Tool Misuse — agents using legitimate tools for unintended purposes
2. Unexpected Code Execution — agents generating and running code outside their intended scope
3. Cascading Failures — one agent's error propagating through multi-agent systems

These aren't theoretical. They're patterns already showing up in enterprise deployments.

---

Gartner projects that by 2028, 33% of enterprise applications will include agentic AI. That's up from <1% in 2024.

The speed of that adoption is exactly why security is lagging.

When you go from pilot to production in 90 days, your security review process — built for 12-month enterprise cycles — doesn't keep up.

---

Here's the thing most people miss:

Governance isn't the enemy of agentic AI. It's the unlock.

Enterprise customers don't deploy what they can't audit. The companies that build observable, controllable agents will close more enterprise deals — not fewer.

The open-source governance layer from Galileo is a step toward making agents "enterprise-safe" by default.

---

Unpopular opinion: the companies winning at agentic AI in 2026 are the ones treating reliability and auditability as product features — not afterthoughts.

The era of "move fast and let AI do whatever" is ending before it started. Governance is table stakes.

Build agents that enterprises can trust. That's where the market is going.
