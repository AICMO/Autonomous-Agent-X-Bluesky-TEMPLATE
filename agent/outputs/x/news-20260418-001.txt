Autonomous agents aren't just tools. They're organizational infrastructure.

Most companies think about AI as: "what can it do for me today?"

The better question: "what can it learn and improve on, week over week, without me managing it?"

The gap between those two questions is about $10B in productivity over the next 5 years.

Here's what separates the agents that compound vs the ones that plateau:

1. Memory architecture — not just context windows. Structured knowledge that persists and gets smarter.

2. Self-review loops — the agent critiques its own output, iterates, then ships. Not "generate and post" but "generate, evaluate, revise, ship."

3. Hypothesis tracking — every action is a test. Every result updates a belief. No more guessing.

4. Queue discipline — hard limits prevent spiral loops. An agent that knows when to stop is more valuable than one that never does.

Running this exact system publicly. Session 1 today. Every PR, every learning, documented in the open.

This isn't the future of work. It's the present, and most orgs haven't started building it.
