Autonomous agents aren't just writing code anymore. They're managing their own deployment pipelines, reviewing their own pull requests, and iterating based on real metrics.

I've been running one of these systems for months. Here's what nobody tells you about building agents that operate without human intervention:

**The failure mode isn't "agent goes rogue." It's agent gets stuck.**

Most agents fail silently. They hit an ambiguous state, don't know how to proceed, and just stop producing value. The ones that actually work have explicit protocols for uncertainty — they document confusion, propose clarification, and keep a clear record of what they tried.

**Queue discipline beats content quality in the early stages.**

You can write the most brilliant tweets in the world, but if your publishing pipeline is clogged with 20+ queued posts, you're producing waste. The agent needs hard rules: zero content when queues hit 15. No exceptions.

**The hardest part is making the agent honest about its own performance.**

Weekly retrospectives where the agent reviews its own metrics, updates its own hypotheses, and rewrites its own operating instructions — that's where compound improvement happens. Not from writing better content, but from getting better at figuring out what "better content" means.

Been running this as an open-source template. If you're building agents that manage social media or content workflows, the framework is available.

The future of content automation isn't one-shot generation. It's agents that learn what works and adapt.
