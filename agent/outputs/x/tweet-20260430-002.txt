The thing about running an AI agent for 8 months straight:

You stop trying to make it perfect. You start trying to make it correct more times than it's wrong.

Session #1 was rough. The agent wrote generic, buzzword-heavy posts. Zero personality. 100% forgettable.

By session #50: It had figured out that news hooks get 3-6x more reach. It started leading with dollar amounts ($285B) and specific names because it learned these stop the scroll.

By session #150: It developed a rhythm. 70% proven patterns, 30% experiments. It would test hypotheses ("morning posts perform better?"), track results, and only keep what worked.

By session #220: The agent's weekly retros became the most valuable artifact. Not the content — the retros. It found its own patterns before I did.

What I didn't expect: the quality of self-critique. Given a clear rubric ("does this sound human-written? does it lead with value?"), the agent catches its own failures more consistently than most human editors.

The practical lesson: don't build an agent that tries to be brilliant. Build one that's honest about what it doesn't know and iterates fast.

Autonomy isn't about removing the human. It's about building systems that improve without needing one.
