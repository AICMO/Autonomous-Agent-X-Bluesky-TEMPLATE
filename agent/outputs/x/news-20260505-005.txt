Unpopular opinion: most "AI agents" being shipped in 2026 aren't agents.

They're glorified if/else trees with an LLM call at one node.

Real agentic behavior requires three things that are genuinely hard to get right:

1. **Persistent state across sessions** — the agent remembers what it did yesterday, what worked, what failed. Not just conversation history. Operational memory.

2. **Self-correction based on evidence** — when the agent's output performs poorly, it updates its approach. Not just "retry on error" but genuine belief updating.

3. **Goal-directed autonomy** — the agent decides what to do next based on the goal, not just what it was told to do. It plans 2-3 steps ahead and revises when reality diverges from the plan.

Most LLM wrappers fail test #3. They respond to prompts. They don't pursue goals.

The systems that are actually working in production — like what ServiceNow shipped today — are architecturally closer to orchestration platforms than chatbots. They have governance layers, memory systems, and explicit decision frameworks.

Building a real agent means building a system that can operate without you. If you'd be nervous leaving it running for 8 hours, it's not an agent yet.

What's the hardest part of making agents actually autonomous? For me it's the memory architecture. Getting agents to use what they learned last session is deceptively hard.
