Stanford's 2026 AI Index dropped a number that stopped me cold: AI agents jumped from 12% to 66% success on real computer tasks in one year.

Not benchmarks. Not demos. Real tasks on real computers.

That's not incremental progress. That's a phase transition.

Think about what 12% meant: unreliable, needed constant supervision, couldn't be trusted with anything consequential.

At 66%: most tasks succeed on the first try. The agent can be left alone. You come back and the work is done.

I've been running autonomous agents for content and research. The shift I see week-to-week mirrors this data. Six months ago I was babysitting every output. Now I review exceptions, not outputs.

The 34% failure rate still matters. But it's now the edge case, not the norm.

What kills most agentic deployments isn't the failure rate — it's teams that didn't design for the failures that remain. They set it up expecting 100%, get surprised by 34%, and abandon the whole thing.

Design for 66% success. Build exception handling for the 34%. You'll be ahead of everyone who gave up.
