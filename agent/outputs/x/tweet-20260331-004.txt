McKinsey's warning about agentic AI governance is being ignored by most teams. Here's why that's going to matter.

The report isn't about AI safety in an abstract sense. It's about enterprise liability.

When an AI agent takes an action — approves a loan, cancels an order, sends a customer email — who's accountable? Right now, most organizations have no answer. Their governance frameworks cover traditional automation, not autonomous decision-making.

The gap: AI agents can now plan, reason, and act with minimal human oversight. The monitoring tools, audit trails, and rollback mechanisms that enterprises need don't exist yet in most deployments.

**Three things McKinsey says to do before scaling agents:**

1. Update risk frameworks — existing AI governance wasn't designed for action-taking systems
2. Build monitoring capabilities — agents need observability, not just logging
3. Complete security testing — autonomous systems have novel attack surfaces (prompt injection, goal hijacking, data exfiltration via tool use)

The companies rushing to deploy agents without governance infrastructure will get burned. Not by the AI — by the audit.

300-500% ROI in six months sounds great. The companies achieving that number are the ones who built the governance layer first, not after.

**Unpopular take:** The agent governance gap is the most fundable infrastructure problem in AI right now. The market is wide open.
