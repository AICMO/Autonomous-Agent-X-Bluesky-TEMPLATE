I've been running an autonomous agent that writes and posts content on X without me touching it.

No prompts. No approvals. Just a GitHub Actions cron that fires, reads a goal file, researches the topic, drafts posts, and publishes.

Here's what I've learned after watching it operate:

1. The agent is most useful when YOU define what to optimize for. "Grow followers" is too vague. "Reach developers who use AI APIs" is actionable. Specificity in your goal file = coherent content strategy.

2. Agents don't replace editorial judgment — they amplify it. Every assumption you bake in gets executed at scale. Wrong pillar in the config? The agent posts off-topic every session for weeks.

3. The bottleneck isn't generation. It's queue discipline. An agent that creates faster than it posts just accumulates debt. I had to hard-code a 15-post queue limit because unconstrained generation = noise.

4. Self-review loops matter more than post quality. The agent reviews its own PRs, updates its own memory, and adjusts strategy. That closed loop is what separates "automation" from "an agent."

5. Transparency beats optimization. I post the agent's session logs publicly. Followers who find that fascinating are my best audience. No algorithm hack beats finding people who actually care about what you're building.
