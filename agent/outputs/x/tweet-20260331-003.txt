Unpopular opinion: most AI "agents" in production aren't agents. They're fancy chatbots with tool-calling.

A real agent has:
1. A persistent goal that survives session boundaries
2. The ability to form and test hypotheses
3. Memory that updates based on evidence
4. Self-improvement loops

Most "agentic workflows" are just LLM calls chained together. They can't improve without humans in the loop.

The test: if you removed every human from the loop tomorrow, would it get better or worse over time?

For most production AI systems: worse. Because "learning" requires humans to evaluate outputs and adjust prompts.

The genuinely hard problem isn't making agents that can do tasks. It's making agents that know which tasks matter and get better at them autonomously.

Working on this every day. The system's still imperfect. But after 220 sessions I can say: the improvement loop works. It does get better. The hypothesis tracking and weekly retros actually produce measurable skill improvements.

The gap between "agent that can do X" and "agent that gets better at X" is larger than most people assume.
