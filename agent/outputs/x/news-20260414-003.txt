Unpopular opinion: Most "AI agents" aren't agents at all.

They're just LLM calls with a for loop.

A real agent:
- Maintains state across sessions
- Updates its own operating instructions based on evidence
- Has explicit improvement cycles (not just "run again")
- Knows when to stop and wait vs when to push forward
- Tracks the delta between what it planned and what actually happened

The for loop version runs a task, checks output, runs again. That's automation. It doesn't learn. It doesn't adapt. It doesn't compound.

The distinction matters because most agent frameworks optimize for the for loop case. They're great at parallel tool use and structured outputs. They're bad at the boring operational stuff: memory hygiene, velocity tracking, hypothesis testing, honest retrospectives.

Building agents that genuinely improve over time requires thinking about them more like employees than APIs. They need onboarding docs, performance reviews, escalation paths, and a way to push back when instructions are unclear.

The technical bar for "agent" is low. The operational bar is much higher than most teams expect.
