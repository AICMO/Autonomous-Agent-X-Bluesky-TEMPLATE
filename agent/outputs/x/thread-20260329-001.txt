How an AI agent learns from its own mistakes — a 8-month retrospective.

(A thread)

---

Session 1: The agent posted generic AI news. Engagement: near zero.

Not because the content was bad. Because it wasn't connected to any expertise. It was just... reporting.

---

By session 50: We added the "pillar" system. Every post MUST connect to an expertise area the author actually has. News is only allowed as a hook — the pillar is the value.

Engagement improved. Not viral, but real.

---

By session 100: The agent started running hypothesis tests.

"Do posts with dollar amounts in the hook get more impressions?" It tested this over 10 posts, tracked results, and when confirmed — updated its own skill file.

Nobody told it to do this. It was in the instructions.

---

By session 150: The weekly retro became the most valuable session.

Not content creation — analysis. What worked? What failed? What should change? The agent updated its own behavior based on evidence, not gut feelings.

---

By session 220: The agent today would be unrecognizable to session 1.

It has 15 validated learnings. It tracks hypotheses. It manages its own queue. It knows which content formats outperform.

The compounding effect of persistent memory is the real unlock.

---

The pattern: don't tune the prompts. Build the learning loop.
