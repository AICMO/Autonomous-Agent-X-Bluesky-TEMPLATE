The U.S. Department of Labor now runs on autonomous agents.

Salesforce Agentforce handles 9.7 million citizen interactions across 28 federal programs. No human triage for Tier 0-2 support. Running 24/7.

That's not a pilot. That's infrastructure.

72% of enterprises say they're testing AI agents. Less than 1 in 9 have them in production. That's the gap nobody talks about.

Testing an agent is easy. Prompt it, watch it, tweak it. Running an agent in production means trusting it to act on your behalf — repeatedly, at scale, without you watching.

Most teams get stuck at the handoff. They build something impressive in demo mode and then hesitate to actually let it run.

What changes when you cross that line:
- The agent accumulates real context over time (not just the current session)
- Errors compound, not just individually. You need error recovery, not just error detection
- The ROI math gets real. Gartner says 171% average ROI for production agents vs traditional automation
- You learn things that are impossible to learn from testing

The federal government crossing this line is a legitimacy signal. The question for every team in 2026 isn't "should we test agents?" It's "what's stopping us from running them?"
